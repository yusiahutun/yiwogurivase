The real story of HBO's 'The Gilded Age'

April 26, 2024
blog

The end of the 19th century in America, also known as the Gilded Age, is endlessly fascinating to modern eyes. So it’s with excitement that fans look forward to the second season of HBO’s hit costume drama, The Gilded Age, which drops on 29 October.

Created by Julian Fellowes (the mastermind behind Downtown Abbey), this season of The Gilded Age is a visually spectacular tale “of fierce rivalry between old money and new,” says HBO, as a nouveau riche family try to break into the highest of high New York society.

This was an era when a handful of grand dames ruled New York, dominating exclusive parties with their legendary extravagance. My latest novel, The Birdcage Library, follows an exotic animal dealer’s wife as she climbs this slippery social ladder. The research took me from costume archives to taxidermy collections, via some of the wildest mansions ever built. Here's what I learnt about the real Gilded Age in the process.

What was the Gilded Age?

The Gilded Age, which lasted from the 1870s to the early 1900s, took its name from a satirical novel by Mark Twain and Charles Dudley Warner, which poked fun at the greed and political corruption in post-Civil War America. America’s population doubled and industry came into its own due to the likes of the Vanderbilts, Carnegies and Rockefellers, who made their names with shipping, steel oil and railroad empires. It was a time of immense wealth – for a few.

Why was its social scene so famous?

The epicentre of America was New York, and its highest social circle was known as the ‘Four Hundred’: the number that could fit into Caroline Schermerhorn Astor’s ballroom. Mrs Astor was this era’s ‘undisputed queen’, an image she played up to: her magnificent jewellery collection, for example, included a piece owned by Marie Antoinette.

But then the nouveaux riches arrived. Old-money ‘Knickerbocker’ society (the name given to the almost-exclusively descendants of British and Dutch aristocratic families that governed the early 1600s American Colonies) had long been a sober affair, and membership was a matter of lineage. That began to change with the new arrivals: freshly minted from oil, steamboats and railroads, and led by the Vanderbilts. At the time, Tiffany’s, sensing opportunity, opened a heraldry department to design coats of arms for would-be aristocrats. Mrs Astor’s defence was to implement unseen codes to trap the unaware pretenders to society thrones: anything from the right costume for croquet, to the correct manner of slicing fruit could trip you up.

Who were some of the real figures involved?

Mrs Astor’s righthand man was Ward McAllister: it was he who coined the phrase ‘The Four Hundred’ and helped Mrs Astor decide who was ‘in’ and who was ‘out’.

Also important were the women who succeeded to Mrs Astor’s throne, known as the ‘Triumvirate’: Mamie Fish, a socialite and self-styled ‘fun-maker’ who, along with her husband Stuyvesant Fish, maintained stately homes in New York and Newport; Theresa Fair Oelrichs, whose father discovered the largest-ever single deposit of gold and silver; and Alva Vanderbilt, a multi-millionaire and women's suffrage activist. Mamie Fish in particular became something of a legend, known for her sharp tongue. “Here you are again, older faces and younger clothes,” she is said to have remarked to guests one year.

Where did they live?

The society glitterati of the Gilded Age lived mostly in huge Fifth Avenue mansions, often built in the style of French chateaux. One (actually French) journalist called the style one of ‘mad abundance’. Mrs Astor’s home included rugs made from the skins of tigers, leopards and polar bears; “no merely useful thing is permitted,” wrote popular magazine Scribner’s Monthly of this tendency for excess for excess’ sake.

There were also massive summer mansions in Newport – ironically referred to as ‘cottages’ – where scenes for season two of The Gilded Age have been filmed. One such house, the Breakaways, built by Cornelius Vanderbilt, has platinum-leafed wall panels and chandeliers of Baccarat crystal.

What about the famous parties?

The pinnacle of the Gilded Age was the Vanderbilt Masked Ball, when Alva Vanderbilt defeated Caroline Astor to throw the party of the century, by which she forced her way into high society. An entire floor of her Gothic-inspired, Fifth Avenue house, the ‘Petit Chateau’ (which took up a whole city block), was turned into a forest featuring palm trees and orchids. One guest dressed as a pharaoh, another as an electric light; the dress of one ‘duchess’ sported real sapphires and emeralds, and a socialite wore a costume made of dead cats.

As I discovered in my research, the Gilded Age loved exotic animals: Mamie Fish once held a party for a ‘prince’ who turned out to be a monkey; for another, she hired an elephant. Meanwhile, plume hunters killed millions of birds for women to wear on their hats – the feathers of the popular egret became worth more than their weight in gold.

Parties were also held at exclusive restaurants like Delmonico’s (inventor of the Baked Alaska), attended by everyone from JP Morgan to Oscar Wilde. A single such dinner involved an artificial lake in the centre of a massive oval table, complete with exotic plants and waterfalls, swans, and a gold wire cage from Tiffany’s.

It was, in short, a spectacular time – but there was a dark side to the glamour, rife as it was with inequality, excess and petty cruelty. As society novelist Edith Wharton wrote, “A frivolous society can acquire dramatic significance only through what its frivolity destroys”.
